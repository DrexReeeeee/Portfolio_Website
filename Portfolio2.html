Data, Information, and Information Systems

Discover the essentials of data, information, and information systems, and their role in transforming raw data into meaningful insights.

What is Data?

> Data are raw facts, statistics, observations, or measurements, frequently in numerical form, that have been collected, recorded, and kept in a database or another system (Kimball & Ross, 2002).

> It exists in different formats, such as, numbers, characters, symbols, visuals, sounds, and measures (Zemmouchi-Ghomari, 2021). It is frequently utilized in research and corporate settings to support conclusions and influence strategic decisions.

> Data has no meaning. They gain meaning only when appropriated by a human recipient (Sanders, 2016).

What is Information?

Information consists of meaningful symbols or signs that can lead to the creation of knowledge. It is data with context and significance (Zins, 2007).

It serves to reduce uncertainty by providing insights, facts, and understanding that help individuals make informed decisions and gain knowledge.

Cambridge International Examinations (2015) state that information is the end product of data processing. This produces facts, allowing the processed data to be used in context and with meaning.

Information Systems

What is an Information System?

> An information system (IS) is a collection of interconnected components that gather, process, store, and distribute data to assist organizational decision making and control (Bourgeois et al., 2019).

> Information systems are like digital nervous systems for organizations. They are the backbone of modern enterprises, enabling everything from customer service to supply chain management.

> Over the last few decades, information systems have enabled flexible manufacturing, e-commerce, and social networking, and they are expected to continue to drive innovation in our knowledge economy (Hsu, 2013).

Components of Information System

The "Leavitt Diamond" model

1. Technology: Includes hardware, software, and telecommunications necessary for managing data.

2. People: Involves managers, users, and IT developers.

3. Task: Activities needed to create goods/services, supported by the exchange of information.

4. Structure: Organizational frameworks, roles, and assessment systems.

IMPORTANCE OF INFORMATION SYSTEMS

Ragowsky and Neumann (1996) emphasize the critical significance that information systems play in modern companies. They mention a few crucial areas where information systems add substantial value and importance:

1. Operational Efficiency: Information systems simplify operations, minimize manual duties, and improve accuracy, resulting in increased operational efficiency.

2. Informed Decision Making: Provides vital facts and insights to help make strategic decisions and improve organizational performance.

3. Resource Managements: Facilitates effective resource allocation and management, resulting in better task planning and execution.

4. Competitive Advantage: Helps firms remain competitive by promoting innovation, increasing customer service, and optimizing business processes.

5. Investment Challenges: Despite considerable technological investments, managers frequently struggle to forecast which information systems applications will provide the most advantages.

Different Types of Support Systems in Information Systems

TRANSACTION PROCESSING SYSTEM

TPS are essential for businesses, managing recurring transactions such as order processing, billing, payroll, and inventory. They ensure smooth operations by efficiently handling a large volume of transactions (Al-Mamary et al., 2014).

Users: Operational staff, finance personnel, and IT teams.

Examples: Payroll systems, inventory systems, accounts payable systems (Mahar, 2003).

Roles: Ensures efficient data handling, supports operations, and tracks financial records (Al-Mamary et al., 2014).

MANAGEMENT INFORMATION SYSTEM

MIS provides managers with reports and insights to support decision-making.

Users: Managers, data analysts, and IT staff (Laudon & Laudon, 2014).

Examples: Sales management and human resource systems, and financial reporting tools.

Roles: Aggregates data, monitors performance, and enhances strategic decisions.

DECISION SUPPORT SYSTEMS

DSS aid in complex decision-making by providing relevant data, analytical tools, and models. They help managers make decisions in situations where the outcomes are uncertain or complex..

Users: Decision-makers, data analysts, and IT staff.

Examples: Financial planning tools, logistics systems, and marketing analysis platforms (Fernando & Baldelovar, 2022).

Roles: Performs complex analysis, simulates scenarios, and supports informed decisions (Rashidi et al., 2018).

EXECUTIVE INFORMATION SYSTEMS

EIS provides senior executives with high-level data and performance metrics.

Users: Senior executives, IT managers, and data specialists (Creps & O’Leary, 1994).

Examples: Executive dashboards and strategic planning and business intelligence tools.

Roles: Delivers strategic overviews, identifies trends, and supports high-impact decisions.
